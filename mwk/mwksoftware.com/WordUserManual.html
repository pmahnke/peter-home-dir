Melt Stock Costing

 

Other parts of this manual deal with the problem of how to use the scrap you currently have on hand in the most cost efficient manner.  Least cost charge calculations merely tell you how to charge your furnace to a given chemistry for the least cost given the scrap you own at the time.  Melt stock costing provides an additional and much more significant way to control costs in your foundry.  The time to save money is when money changes hands.  If you can buy more cost effective melt stocks to begin with, you have an additional way to save money. 

 

As you have used the charge calculation program, you have undoubtedly generated recipes that looked strange to you.  A common reaction is to say, you can't make this alloy from that scrap.  Provided you have entered reasonable chemistries and costs, the unusual recipes are the least cost way to make the charge.  The program is merely able to keep track of more details and is not constrained to do things the way we've always done them.  Now that you have acclimated yourself to new recipes, the time has come to buy more cost effective scrap.

 

Melt stock costing provides several tools to help you buy more cost effective scrap.  A good starting point is to use simple charge calculations to experiment with scrap you know is available but have never purchased because you weren't sure how to use it.  Using a furnace range and the melt stocks you normally have on hand, enter the cost and chemistry of a proposed purchase.  If the subsequent calculation uses some of the proposed melt stock, then not only do you now know how to use it, but you have also reduced your total melt stock cost.  Now you might try varying the cost of the proposed melt stock to see just how much you could pay for it and still use it.

 

Melt stock costing does these calculations for you in a more convenient manner that should encourage you to use the calculations regularly.  You should use the charge calculation program more for melt stock purchasing than for charging your furnace.  Melt stock costing produces two graphs showing various purchase prices for a melt stock and the resulting usage in a charge and resulting per pound / kilogram costs and two reports showing melt stock usage in furnace ranges and relative melt stock values.

 

Melt Stock Costing Graphs

 

For every melt stock, there is a price at which it is cheaper to make the charge without using the melt stock.  Below this cost, the melt stock will be used at varying amounts depending on its cost.

 

The two melt stock costing graphs show the usage for the selected melt stock.  To produce the first melt stock costing graph, the program performs many charge calculations and summarizes the results of the calculations in a graph.

 

The second melt stock costing graph is an enlargement of any portion of the first graph.  The graph is centered on the current cost of the melt stock.  The graph extends plus or minus 5 times the entry for the cost increment.

 

To do a melt stock costing graph, you need to select a furnace range and optionally, additional constraints.  You also need to select the melt stocks you wish to consider in the charge.  From these melt stocks you will select one for costing.  You also need to make an entry for print charge calculations and charge weight.  Choose either the type one or type two graph. Enter a cost increment for the cost two graph.

 

Some elements may not be found in the other melt stocks.  For instance if the only source of silicon is ferro silicon (the unlikely assumption that no melt stocks have any silicon in them) then there is no substitute for ferro silicon at any cost.  The graph will begin at the cost of the highest priced melt stock.  More likely, is the situation where none of the melt stocks have enough silicon (or other element) in them to meet the minimum furnace range requirement.  In this case ferro silicon (or other element) will be used no matter what its cost.

 

Another possibility is that a melt stock will not be used in a charge no matter what its cost, even at zero cost.  In this case, a message will be printed at the bottom of the screen and one charge report will be printed.  This charge report is calculated at zero cost for the melt stock in question and will show no usage of the melt stock.

 

The purpose of melt stock costing is to assist the melt stock buyer in deciding what melt stocks to buy.  Based on the furnace range you want to make and current inventory, you can easily decide which melt stocks available to you are the best value.

 

The first screen to appear when you select melt stock costing is the melt stock access screen.  You will use this screen to select the melt stock you wish to cost.  The melt stocks that have been selected for inclusion in the charge calculation are indicated with an X in the left hand column.  The melt stock selected for costing is indicated in the message at the bottom of the screen.  You cannot add, modify or delete melt stocks at this time.  You also cannot change the melt stocks selected for inclusion in the charge calculation.

 

  Possible error messages are:

 

  No furnace range selected.  Select a Furnace Range.

 

  No melt stock selected.  Select a Melt Stock for costing

 

  Melt stock not selected for charge calculation.  The selected melt stock must be among those selected for inclusion in the charge calculation.  See prior error message.

 

  Number of pounds or kilograms required is zero.  Enter a non-zero value for charge weight.

Melt Stock Costing Reports

 

Where Used Report

 

The first melt stock costing report shows the usage of the selected melt stock in every furnace range.  The resulting charge cost is also shown.  This report is useful when you have a melt stock available for purchase and need to know where you can use it.  You can restrict this report to only selected furnace ranges. The report calculates a least cost charge calculation for every furnace range that is selected. Restricting the number of furnace ranges that are selected will reduce the time needed to calculate the report.

 

The second melt stock costing report shows calculated costs for each element and calculated melt stock values.  The first part of the report shows the average cost per pound / kilogram for each element.  This part of the report can be compared over time to show if certain elements are more or less expensive.  The second part of the report uses the calculated element costs to produce a calculated value for each melt stock.  Melt stocks that have a calculated value greater than their cost are flagged with a +.  This part of the report will help you identify why certain melt stocks are being used in charge calculations and why others are not being used.  The only melt stocks used in this report are those selected with an X and have a non-zero on hand amount.

Specific Charge Calculations

 

The recipe evaluation function allows you to select specific melt stocks and specific weights for those melt stocks and calculate the resulting chemistry.  To use this function, you must have selected a furnace range and optionally, additional constraints.  You may select up to 24 melt stocks. 

 

The next screen to appear is the recipe evaluation request screen.  You will enter the specific weights for the selected melt stocks on this screen.  When you are finished, click on Continue and the resulting chemistry will be calculated. 

 

 

Power User Charge Calculations

 

The first screen in the program is where you select the data you wish to use in a charge calculation, melt stocks, a furnace range, the weight of the charge and possibly additional constraints and groups of melt stocks. When you click on Continue, the report for the charge calculation is shown on the reports screen. You will find a command button on this screen called Power Calculations. When you click on this button, you will be transferred to a screen that shows the most recent charge calculation results and also allows you to select and deselect melt stocks. A new least cost charge is calculated every time you select or deselect a melt stock. This allows you to quickly see how the results will change as you change the melt stocks that are selected for a charge calculation.

 

Note, you can not change anything else for the charge calculations except the melt stocks that are selected. When you need to make other changes to the information used in a charge calculation, click on the Return button and you will be returned to the charge calculation screen.

 

Final Additions / Back Charge Calculations

 

When calculating a charge you specify what melt stocks to use, required additional constraints, a furnace range and a target furnace weight.  The melt stocks are normally everything you have available to put in the furnace.  This includes purchased scrap, returns and relatively expensive bimetallic and pure furnace additions.  Additional constraints may be specified for back charge calculations just as they are for charge calculations.

 

A back charge, final addition, trim or alloy addition calculation also tells you what to put in the furnace with three significant differences.  The first difference is that the major melt stock is the current contents of the furnace.  The calculation must use all of the current furnace.

 

The second difference is that there is no target total weight for a back charge calculation.  The weight you enter is the current weight of the furnace.  The back charge calculation calculates the least expensive melt stocks required to bring your furnace back to the required chemistry.  There is no restraint on the resulting total weight of the furnace.

 

The third difference is the melt stocks that will be considered.  Although it may be cheaper to bring your furnace back to chemistry by using purchased scrap and returns, practice requires using bimetallic and pure furnace additions.  The reason is that furnace additions will result in a lower total furnace weight and furnace additions are easier to weigh and add to the furnace.  Therefore, when calculating back charges you should restrict the possible melt stocks to binary metallic and pure furnace additions.

 

Because you can select melt stocks with an H, which indicates that the melt stock is a furnace heel and exactly the on hand weight of the melt stock will be used in the calculation, you may be tempted to select your current furnace chemistry with an H and request a charge calculation when you really want a back charge calculation.  The resulting calculation will not be what you want.  Remember, a charge calculation has a target weight and a back charge calculation does not.  Select the chemistry of your current furnace by entering a melt stock and selecting it with a C.

 

Back Charge Calculations - Integrated With A Spectrometer

 

When you select a back charge calculation, the average chemistry of your sample is automatically passed to the back charge calculation.  It is put in the melt stock inventory with the name you gave to the sample and it is selected with a C.  If you analyze for elements that are not used by the back charge calculation, the concentrations of these elements are added to the concentration for the matrix element.  For instance if you analyze for boron on your spectrometer but don't consider boron when calculating back charges, the concentration of boron will be added to the concentration of the matrix element.

 

The Back Charge Calculation

 

Before you can specify a back charge calculation, you must select the melt stocks and additional constraints you will use.  You also must select the furnace range you want.  The melt stocks and additional constraints stay selected from one calculation to the next.  The current furnace melt stock is automatically selected in the integrated mode.

 

The selected furnace range also stays selected from calculation to calculation.  You need to reselect a furnace range only if you change furnace ranges from one sample to the next.

 

The number of melt stocks and additional constraints selected and the selected furnace range are shown at the top of the screen.  The only entry you must make is the number of pounds / kilograms in the furnace.  The other information on the screen is optional.

 

The number of pounds / kilograms you enter represents the weight of the furnace, which is the current furnace melt stock.  The calculation will use exactly that many pounds / kilograms of current furnace melt stock.  You should not enter a recovery ratio for the current furnace melt stock.  Neither should you enter a cost, minimum usage or inventory amount.

 

The back charge calculation will tell you how much of various melt stocks you should add to the furnace to bring the furnace back to the required chemistry.  If the furnace is already correct, no additions will be calculated.

 

The foundry range specification used for a back charge can be the same as the one you used to calculate the initial least cost charge or it can be different. Zero defect manufacturing means reducing the variance of the chemistry of each element to zero. While this is not normally possible, back charge calculations can go a long way in reducing your chemistry variance. If you specify an upper and lower limit for an element, say 8 and 10 percent, then the back charge calculation will merely insure that the chemistry is between 8 and 10 percent. The possibility for variance exists because one charge may have a concentration of 8 and the next may have a concentration of 10. If instead of a range, you enter an aim, then the concentration for the element will be reduced. For instance a lower limit of 8.5 and an upper limit of 8.5 will result in a back charge calculation with a resulting chemistry of 8.5 percent for the element. By using aims for each element you consider, the squiggly lines on your SPC charts can be straightened out.

 

 

 

Groups of Melt Stocks

 

Adding a Group of Melt Stocks

 

New groups are added to the program by clicking on Add a Melt Stock Group.  At this point the groups of melt stocks information maintenance screen is displayed.  You can now enter information for a new group of melt stocks.  You must enter a name for the group.  As with melt stocks, the minimum usage may not be larger than the maximum usage.

 

When you select or deselect a group, all the melt stocks associated with that group are also selected or deselected.  If you delete a melt stock group and it was selected, all the melt stocks associated with that group will be deselected.

 

The number of groups of melt stocks in the program and the number of selected groups of melt stocks are shown at the bottom of the groups of melt stocks access screen.

 

  Possible error messages are:

 

  No name for group.  You must enter a name for the melt stock group.

 

  Minimum usage is greater than maximum usage.  The minimum usage must be less than or equal to the maximum usage.

 

  The maximum usage is zero or greater than 100.

 

When you are finished entering the information for a group of melt stocks, click on Update Information.  If there are no errors, the group will be added to the program and the group of melt stocks access screen will be redisplayed.  If there are any errors, you must fix them or click on Ignore Changes to exit from the screen, in which case all the information you entered will be ignored.

 

Usage Constraints for Groups of Melt Stocks

 

You can easily construct an additional constraint that will limit the total amount of tramp elements in a charge. Each tramp element will be restricted to its limit by the furnace range and the additional constraint will limit the total amount of the tramp elements. Groups of melt stocks do the same thing for melt stock usage.

 

Suppose you have some oily turnings that will smoke when you put them in the furnace. You can specify a maximum usage for each individual turning melt stock, however, if you have several turning melt stocks, the total amount of turnings used in a charge may be excessive.

 

To solve this problem, you can create a melt stock group that has all the turning's melt stocks as members. If the maximum usage for the group is five percent, then a charge calculation will be limited to a total of five percent turnings.

 

When you select the oily turnings melt stock group, all the melt stocks in the group will also be selected. When you deselect the group, all the member melt stocks will be deselected.

 

The minimum usage entry for a melt stock group will force the charge calculation to use at least that percent of the member melt stocks in a calculation. If you make an entry for on hand, then the calculation will be limited to no more than that amount of member melt stocks.

 

Usage Constraints for melt stocks in a group of melt stocks

 

In addition to specifying a maximum and minimum usage percent for a group of melt stocks, you can also specify maximum and minimum usage percents for each melt stock in the group. Each melt stock has a maximum and minimum usage percent that was specified when you entered the melt stock data on the melt stock maintenance screen. When a melt stock is selected as a member of a group, you have the opportunity to specify different maximum and minimum lower usage limits for the melt stock.

 

This means that when a melt stock is part of a group of melt stocks, it can have more than one set of maximum and minimum usage percents, the original percents specified on the melt stock maintenance screen and the percents specified on the groups of melt stocks maintenance screen. When the charge calculation is set up, the maximum and minimum percents specified on the groups of melt stocks screen for the melt stocks are used.

 

An example:

 

Suppose melt stock Good & Cheap has a maximum usage of 99% entered on the melt stock maintenance screen and a minimum usage of 1%. When it is included in the group, Use This Stuff, it gets a maximum usage percent of 95 and a minimum usage percent of 5.

 

If the melt stock Good & Cheap is selected, but not the group of melt stocks, Use This Stuff, then the minimum usage will be 1% and the maximum usage will be 99%. If the group of melt stocks, Use This Stuff, is now selected, the minimum usage percent will be 5 and the maximum usage percent will be 95.

Additional Constraints

 

Additional constraints allow you to define relationships between the assays of the elements. Here are several examples:

 

Tramp element limits

 

Most metal specifications specify an individual limit for various tramp elements.  A total for all of the tramp elements is also specified.  The idea is to set up an additional constraint on the calculation.  For high alloy steel this constraint might be W + V + Nb + Co less than .025.  If the individual limits for these elements is .01, then the total could be as high as .04 which is not acceptable.  The additional constraint will limit the total of the tramp elements to whatever upper limit you specify. 

 

To create a tramp element constraint, simply enter 1.000 for the multiplier for each of the tramp elements and a suitable upper limit.  The lower limit will be zero.  The constant will also be zero.

 

Carbon equivalents

 

Another example is carbon equivalents in gray iron.  The formula C + Si/3 + P/2 = 4.3 is a linear combination of three elements that will give the carbon equivalent in gray iron.  The elements will be kept within their upper and lower bounds defined by the furnace range and the additional constraint formula will also insure that the sum of carbon plus one third of the silicon plus one half of the phosphorus will equal 4.3.

 

To construct this carbon equivalent constraint, enter 1.000 for carbon multiplier, .333 for silicon and .500 for phosphorus.  If the sum must be exactly 4.3 enter 4.300 for both the upper and lower limit.  You might also enter a range with the lower limit equal 4.250 and the upper limit equal 4.350

 

Aluminum Hardness

 

Hardness in aluminum castings is given by the formula Fe + 2 x Mn + 3 x Cr.  A typical value for this formula is 1.5.  To construct this aluminum hardness constraint, enter 1.000 for iron, 2.000 for manganese and 3.000 for chrome.  The upper and lower limit can be equal to 1.5 or you may specify a range of say 1.45 to 1.55.

 

ASTM A 800 - 82, Ferrite Content

 

A more complex example is ferrite content as defined by ASTM A 800 - 82.  The volume percent ferrite is a linear combination of eight elements.  However the example given ignores nitrogen.  Nitrogen was not analyzed for in the melt stocks used in the calculations.  Including nitrogen would merely add one more factor to the formula.

 

From the Schoefer diagram included with standard A 800 - 82 we see that for each volume percent ferrite there is a Cre/Nie composition ratio.  To obtain this ratio, locate the desired volume ferrite on the bottom of the graph and go up to the curve.  At the curve, turn left to find the equivalent ratio.  For example, a volume percent ferrite of 28% translates to a ratio of 1.5.

 

Now using the formula given, the linear combination of elements for the additional constraint may be constructed.  1.5 = ( Cr% + 1.5Si% + 1.4Mo% + Nb% - 4.99 ) / ( Ni% +30C% + 0.5Mn% + 2.77 ).  Expanding the formula we get: 1.5Ni% + 45C% + 0.75Mn% - Cr% - 1.5Si% - 1.4Mo% - Nb% + 9.145 = 0.0.  Which is a linear combination of seven elements.  The numbers in front of the element percents may be plus or minus.  A constant addition of 9.145 is also allowed.  Note that the upper and lower limits are zero.  This constraint will force the linear combination of elements plus the constant to be exactly equal to zero. 

 

ASTM A 255 - 79, Critical Diameters and Hardenability

 

The ASTM standard A 255 - 79 describes hardenability characteristics and ideal critical diameters.  Again this standard can be converted to a linear combination of elements and entered as an additional constraint in the charge calculation. 

 

From table A4 of the standard, a critical diameter is selected and its corresponding sum is found.  A critical diameter of 4 inches has a sum of 1.602.  Customer specifications generally specify a range of critical diameters.  A range could be specified here such as 4 inches to 4 1/2 inches with corresponding sums of 1.602 and 1.653.  Now all that is necessary is to build a linear combination of elements that will give this sum.

 

8630 is a metal covered by this standard.  The upper and lower limits for carbon in the furnace range are .270 and .330.  Now looking at table A3 and selecting a grain size of 7, a formula that relates percent of carbon to the value in the table must be constructed.

 

A linear formula is appropriate as the range in carbon value (.270 to .330) is small.  To obtain the formula, solve the two equations .27a + b = .241 and .330a + b = .288.  The resulting formula is .783C% + .030 = value in table.  The multiplier for carbon in the additional constraint will be .783 and .030 will be part of the additive constant.

 

This procedure was repeated for the rest of the elements and resulted in the following linear combination of elements: .783C% + .409Mn% + .225Si% + 1.1P% - .2S% + .457Cr% + .133Ni% + .830Mo% + .500Al + .407 = 1.602.

 

Density in Aluminum

 

The following additional constraint controls the density of the aluminum used for pistons.  The requirement for aluminum density is given by the following formula:

 

Density = 100 / (0.00681 x Cu + 0.00775 x Fe + 0.03507 x Mg + 0.00821 x Mn + 0.02619 x Si + 0.00856 x Zn + 0.02261 x Al + 0.00872 x residuals) + 0.25

 

Residuals are assumed to be exactly 0.25 and Aluminum = 100 - (Cu + Fe + Mg + Mn + Si + Zn + 0.25).  A density range of 44.83 to 45.06 or 44.88 to 45.01 is allowed with a preferred value of 44.94.  The following is the development of the additional constraint that will result in a least cost charge calculation with the required density of 44.94.

 

44.96 = 100 / (0.00681 x Cu + 0.00775 x Fe + 0.03507 x Mg + 0.00821 x Mn + 0.02619 x Si + 0.00856 x Zn + 0.02261 x Al + 0.00872 x residuals) + 0.25

 

Rearranging terms gives:

 

0.706 x Cu + 0.664 x Fe - 0.557 x Mg + 0.644 x Mn - 0.160 Si + 0.628 x Zn = .889

 

  Using this additional constraint will yield recipes with a density of 44.94.  The specification assumes that residuals are exactly 0.25.  In any given calculation, residuals may be less than 0.25.  In that case, you must adjust the aluminum result to get density values that correspond to the Ford specification.  The formula could be modified to use actual residual values instead of the assumed 0.25.

Not all calculations will result in a valid charge.  It is possible that the furnace range you wish to make can't be made from the selected melt stocks.  Additional constraints may create contradictory requirements.

The given element may be found in one melt stock, but using a sufficient amount of that melt stock may push another element over the maximum allowed in the furnace range.

 

Minimum usage constraints may not be met if the minimum usage would push an element over the maximum allowed in the furnace range.

 

Another cause of invalid calculations is furnace ranges that do not allow any amount of a given element.  If the upper and lower limits for an element are both zero, then any melt stock that has that element in its assay will be excluded from the calculation.  You should allow at least some small value such as 0.005 for all elements.

Gratuitous Melt Stocks and Elements

 

Occasionally a charge calculation will show a small amount of zinc or aluminum or other inexpensive melt stock.  The resulting concentration for that element will be at the top of its furnace range.  This is called a gratuitous melt stock and element.  If you have zinc ingot that is nearly pure and costs less per pound / kilogram than the resulting charge, then the charge calculation will always use as much as possible of the zinc ingot, because it is cheaper to build the charge that way. Another example is iron in aluminum charges. If you have iron filings available at five cents a pound, the program will use as much as possible which will result in an unreasonable recipe. The way to avoid this problem is to use a value of 1.00 for iron filings and a cost of five cents. The program will then use only the smallest possible amount.

 

You can rerun the calculation without the gratuitous melt stock selected.  Or you can ignore the melt stock when charging the furnace.  If you ignore the melt stock then any element at its upper limit will now be over its upper limit.  In practice, this is not a problem as the amount of gratuitous melt stock is generally small.  A specific charge calculation will uncover any problems.

 

Fluxable Magnesium In Aluminum Charges

 

A charge calculation will keep magnesium at or below its upper furnace limit without fluxing.  This may be more expensive than allowing magnesium to exceed its upper limit and fluxing out the excess.  You can use charge calculations to explore the more cost effective approach.

 

Assume that the upper limit for magnesium is 1 percent.  First do a charge calculation with an upper limit of 1 percent and a recovery ratio of 100.  Do a second calculation with the same upper limit of 1 percent but with a recovery ratio of 50 percent.  Add to the cost of the second calculation the cost of fluxing out the excess magnesium.

 

A charge calculation with 2 percent magnesium and a recovery ratio of 100 percent would be inappropriate.  If you charge to 2 percent magnesium and then flux out half of it, the concentrations for the other elements will be wrong.  Namely all elements that were at their upper limits will now exceed their upper limits.  Recovery ratios automatically correct for the assumed loss of the element.

 

Carbon Losses in Arc Furnaces

 

A considerable amount of carbon is consumed in an arc furnace.  The problem is how to handle this carbon loss.  There are three ways to handle losses in the program: overall melt stock losses; elemental losses in melt stocks; and elemental losses in furnace ranges.

 

If a uniform part of the carbon in each melt stock, including the coke is lost, then adjusting the carbon recovery rate in the furnace range would be the best way to handle the problem.  A 10% carbon recovery rate in the furnace range would mean that only 10% of the coke remains after melt down and also that only 10% of the carbon in all of the other melt stocks remains.  This is unlikely.  The vast majority of the carbon loss is from the coke and only lesser rates of loss occur in the melt stocks.

 

If the coke is pure carbon, which it isn't, then an overall recovery loss for the coke melt stock would be appropriate.  The problem here is that a 10% recovery rate for the coke melt stock means that the sulfur is also recovered at only a 10% rate, which unfortunately is not the case.

 

The correct solution is to enter a 10% recovery for the element carbon in the coke melt stock.  This means that only 10% of the carbon in the coke will remain after the melt down.  A slight modification to the furnace range recovery rate for carbon may also be appropriate to reflect the fact that some of the carbon from the other melt stocks is also lost.

A campaign of charges optimizes the usage of melt stocks across several charges.  If you have several good melt stocks that you reserve for particular charges, a campaign of charges will show you if your decision has been a cost effective one.  The campaign of charges will allocate your good melt stocks in the most cost efficient way.

 

A campaign of charges also can be used to expand on the information in the where used report in the melt stock costing reports.  The where used report shows how much of a melt stock would be used in various individual charges.  By requesting a campaign of charges for charges that will be made during a period, both with and without the prospective melt stock purchase, you can see exactly how the prospective purchase would be used in the various charges and how it will affect the total cost of the charges.

 

A campaign of charges optimizes charges that will be made over a period of time.  Some melt stocks that will be used in the later charges may not actually have been delivered at the time of the calculation.  Enter the expected chemistries and costs of these melt stocks and then select them only for the charges that will be made after their delivery.  Melt stocks that are available at the time of the first charge should be selected for the first charge and all subsequent charges.  In this way, the number of available melt stocks will increase for later charges and the program will automatically allocate the melt stocks to the charges in the most cost effective way.

 

The usage constraints for melt stock groups only apply to individual charges within a campaign. If the on hand entry for a group is 5,000 pounds / kilograms, then each charge will be limited to 5,000 pounds / kilograms of melt stocks from that group, but the total usage of those melt stocks may exceed 5,000 pounds.

 

The program is limited to a maximum of 20 charges in a campaign.  If you are making several charges in succession of the same furnace range, then consolidating these charges into one large charge will reduce the number of charges in the campaign.  The recipe for the large charge can then be divided for the smaller charges.

 

Calculation times for a campaign of charges increase at the square of the time required for one charge.  A campaign of 10 charges will require about 100 times as much computing time as one charge

The recovery percent refers to how much of the melt stock is actually usable metal.  If all the melt stock is usable, that is, the recovery percent is 100%, then you do not need to make an entry as the default value for recovery percents is 100.  If the melt stock is mixed with dirt, is painted, or is wet, enter a number from 1 to 99 to reflect the percent of the melt stock that will remain after the contaminant has vaporized or become slag.

The entry for minimum density is only required if you indicated that you wanted to consider minimum densities on the program definition screen. If you are using minimum densities, make an entry in what ever unit of measure you wish. The program will use the density for each melt stock to meet the minimum density entry you specified on the program definition screen.

 

The entry you make for the cost of a melt stock should be what you paid for it. The value entry may be different from the cost entry to reflect faster charging rates or processing. For instance, if you have a melt stock that charges faster than other melt stocks, you can incorporate this difference by making the value entry less than the cost entry. This means that the melt stock is cheaper to use and the difference would be the savings you will realize from its faster charging rate.

 

The entry for value is the number used by the program to solve for the least cost recipe. The melt stock's value is adjusted by the net recovery percent of the melt stock. A melt stock with a value of 0.30 and a recovery of 80% will have a net value of 0.375 (0.30 / 80%). If 100 pounds / kilograms of the melt stock are required, the program will adjust that to 125 pounds / kilograms. The total cost of the recipe is calculated using the cost of each melt stock. The program can assume that cost is equal to value for each melt stock by making an entry on the program definition screen.

 

You also may enter a recovery percent on an element by element basis.  The element by element recovery percent is used to identify losses for each element during the melting process.  An entry of zero for an element indicates that none of that element will survive the melting process.  The element recovery percents in the furnace ranges are used to identify losses after melting, while the metal is in the furnace.

Usage Constraints

 

There are three fields that control usage of a melt stock in a charge calculation.  Without usage constraints, the program will calculate the least cost charge using as much or as little of each melt stock as required..  However, the resulting recipe may not be a usable recipe.  Some melt stocks may be difficult to melt and should be restricted to suitable quantities.  Other melt stocks may not be available in the required amounts, and other melt stocks may be needed as ladle additions.  Usage constraints allow you to force a minimum amount of a melt stock for use as a ladle addition.  Using usage constraints, you can limit the use of a melt stock to a given percent of the total size of the charge.  You also can limit the use of a melt stock to the amount of the melt stock actually available.

 

The first usage constraint field is used to force a minimum usage of a melt stock.  If you make an entry in this field and if you have selected the melt stock for inclusion in a charge calculation, then at least the percent entered of the melt stock will be used in the calculation.  An entry of 5 means at least 5 percent of the charge will be that melt stock.  An entry of zero means that the program will use as little of the melt stock as is required.

 

The maximum field and the on hand field control the maximum usage of a melt stock.  You can enter a value in the maximum field to indicate an upper limit on the amount of the melt stock that will be used.  An entry of 30 will limit use of the melt stock to 30 percent of the charge.  Of course the least cost calculation may require less.  The default value of 100 is supplied if you do not wish to limit the amount of the melt stock used.

 

The on hand field will limit the use of the melt stock to no more than the indicated pounds / kilograms.  Again the least cost calculation may require less.  An entry of 9999999 will result in an entry of NoLimit. When a melt stock has an on hand entry of NoLimit, the program will use as much of the melt stock as is needed. If you relieve inventory after a charge calculation, melt stocks with an entry of NoLimit will not be relieved. The NoLimit entry is useful for final addition melt stocks or other melt stock you always have on hand.

 

An error message will be displayed if the minimum usage percent is greater than the maximum usage percent.  During the charge calculation the number of pounds / kilograms on hand may try to force fewer pounds / kilograms to be used than the minimum usage percent.  Only one of the conditions will be met.  Look at the minimum, maximum and on hand information on the cost report, to spot this kind of error.

Usage Reports

 

Version 6.5 includes usage reports for melt stocks and furnace ranges.

 

When you click on Relieve Inventory after you have done a charge or final additions calculation the program will reduce the amount on hand for each melt stock used in the calculation. The amount used for each melt stock will also be tracked. This means that you can see how much of each melt stock has been used, but only if you click on Relieve Inventory after a charge or final additions calculation. The program also tracks how much of each furnace range ahs been made.

 

You see the values for how much of each melt stock has been used and how much of each furnace range has been made by clicking on Data / Melt Stock Update for melt stock usage and Data / Furnace Range Use for furnace ranges.

 

You may also see a report by clicking on Other reports / Materials Used and Alloys Made. You may print this report and you may also zero out the values when the report is displayed. You might do this each month or week to keep a monthly or weekly report.

 

Evaluating Formulas

 

The Additional Constraints are limited to linear formulas such as Carbon Equivalent, CE = C+1/9 Si + 1/3.5 P, as an example. There is a need to evaluate non linear formulas such as this one from the AFS for White Iron, 1.07 + 0.43(%Ni)+0.44(%Cu) + 0.59(%Mo x %Mn)+0.25(%Cr/%C)-0.25(%Si). A new entry, called Formulas has been made under Data in the menu bar on the main screen.

 

Select the formula you want to evaluate on the Formula Selection Screen. The value of the formula will be printed on the charge calculation report just after the value of any Additional Constraints. If you do not want the formula you selected to be evaluated, then click on No Formula on the Formula Selection Screen.

 

Let us know if you need an additional formula and we will add it to the program.
